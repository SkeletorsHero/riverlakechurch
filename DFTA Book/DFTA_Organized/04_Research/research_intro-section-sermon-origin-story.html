The note that started it all ::

One Sunday I decided to preach on gluttony. I wanted to hit the big issues our culture struggled with and this was one. I preached the sermon and thought it would be like any one other weekend. Man was I was wrong. Two things happened. First there were a group of people who decided to go to Denny's that day, post it on social media, and comment on how well that sermon went over for them. Second, one person got up in the middle of the message, walked down to my office and left me a personal note that read: "Maybe you should spend more time preaching the Gospel instead of picking on people's issues".

At first I was offended. Didn’t they listen to what I had to say? How could they not see how important this issue is? Don’t they see that this issue IS a Gospel issue? Obviously not which is the disconnect for so many in the modern day church. For them, the Gospel is about saving people from eternal fire of hell, but not saving them from the internal fires that burn inside their own hearts.

That day I don’t know what was running through their minds. Maybe they were just mad, maybe there were embarrassed because i called them out, or maybe I just did a poor job of preaching (nah…that can’t be it), or like so many people, maybe they actually did separate the Gospel from their own sins. Which for me brings up a few questions like:
  • Who is the Gospel for and when should you preach it?
  • Is the Gospel for salvation only? If so when does that salvation start?
  • If we are forgiven of all of our sins which ones should we take seriously enough to change?

The Gospel According to Paul ::

This all leads us to Paul. In the letter to the Roman church Paul speaks to the Gospel frequently. In the first chapter he mentions something so contrary to what most churches speak about when they talk about the Gospel. In verse 8 Paul praises their great faith that is known throughout the world at the time. This means they were already followers of Jesus and seem to be doing quite well at it if everyone knows how well they are doing. But in verse 15 Paul tells them he can't wait to come see them so he can preach the Gospel to them.

But wait, I thought they were already Christians? And good ones at that! And if they are already Christians, shouldn't they already know the Gospel? And if they know the Gospel, have accepted the Gospel, and are living out...why would Paul be excited to preach it to them again? This makes no sense if the sole purpose of the Gospel is to lead people salvation so they don't go to hell.

But what if the Gospel is meant to be more. What if the Gospel, the good news of who Jesus is, is meant to continually challenge who you are and what you are doing throughout your whole life. What if the Gospel is central to all change a person needs. What if the reason the person who struggle with gluttony and wrote me the note about the Gospel struggled because they didn't understand that Gospel. What if the Gospel is meant not just to save us one day some day from the sting of death but also save this day from the sting of sin. What if being a follower of Jesus is more than believing in something and is about following someone, including what and how much to eat.

Back to the basics ::

I'm going to say something maybe a little radical. Jesus doesn't just want to change your mailing address in the afterlife. He wants to change this life. To follow Jesus means that salvation comes this day as much as one day someday! There are two words you should know about when it comes to your faith. Sanctification and Justification. Both are important when one becomes a Christian. Justification is what happens immediately when you repent and put your faith in Christ. This means we are justified before our Heavenly Father through Christ. This can never been changed or undone. Once you are saved you are saved...if you could reverse then you weren't really saved but being saved...which isn't what scripture tells us.

The other word though is a little different. While Justification is instant, sanctification is a process. It happens slowly over time and is how we change into who God calls us to be. Some seasons we change faster and more than others.
