# DFTA — Don't Feed The Alligators
## Master Reference Document
*Author: Nic Smith | Last updated: April 15, 2026*

---

## What This Book Is About

**"Don't Feed the Alligators"** is a Christian self-help book that uses the metaphor of alligators — things we feed and allow to grow in our lives — to explore the 7 Deadly Sins (plus Vainglory, making 8 total). The central argument is that our battles are never *outside* us; they are always *inside* us. Until we deal with what is feeding our inner life, no outward behavior change will stick.

The book's thesis can be summarized in three words: **"Don't Feed It."** Whatever is slowly consuming you — pride, envy, greed, anger, sloth, gluttony, lust, or vainglory — you have been feeding it. This book shows you what you're feeding, why you keep feeding it, and how to stop.

The origin story: Nic preached a sermon on gluttony, expected a normal Sunday, and instead witnessed two remarkable reactions — a group publicly mocked the sermon by posting their Denny's meal on social media, and another group had the most profound spiritual encounter they'd had in years. That contrast revealed something: this topic is alive. People either laugh it off or it cuts to the bone. This book leans into the cut.

---

## Core Metaphor

A family on vacation in Daytona sees a sign: *"Feed and Hold Live Gators — Now Hiring."* They pay to feed tiny, foot-long baby alligators. It feels harmless, even fun. But alligators don't stay small. The things we feed — bad habits, destructive emotions, secret sins — start small and manageable. We convince ourselves we can handle them. Then one day, they bite.

**Lake Placid ending** (for the final chapter): In the movie, the heroes kill the monster gator. Peace is restored. But the final shot is of a sweet old lady still feeding new ones at the water's edge. The real threat was never the gator — it was the person who kept feeding it.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Chapter 1 — Don't Feed the Alligators
**Core idea:** We all feed things we shouldn't. The question isn't whether you have alligators in your life — it's whether you're still feeding them. A thought leads to an action, an action to a habit, a habit to character, character to destiny (Chinese proverb). The baby gator you feed today is the monster that bites you tomorrow.

**Key illustration:** Family feeding baby gators in Daytona. Comfortable with what seems manageable.

**Chapter arc:** ME (vacation story) → WE (we all do this) → GOD (what does God say?) → YOU (what do you do?)

---

### Chapter 2 — Band-aids Don't Fix Bullet Holes
**Core idea:** Behavioral change alone doesn't work because behavior is only a *symptom*. The disease is in the heart. Jesus confronts the Pharisees in Matthew 15: it's not what goes *into* a person that defiles them, but what comes *out*. Band-aids on cancer won't cure it.

**Key illustration:** Nic using the "B word" (BITTER) in an argument with his wife Lisa — a word he promised never to use. The word was just the symptom of weeks of unprocessed anger.

**Taylor Swift quote:** "Band-aids don't fix bullet holes."

---

### Chapter 3 — Pride: The One to Rule Them All
**Core idea:** Pride is the root sin — the one that feeds all others. It's what got Satan cast out of heaven. Pride says *I know better*. Pride chooses being right over being righteous. It shows up subtly: rejecting advice we know we should take, needing to win every argument, treating our preferences as facts.

**Key illustration:** Nic asking his 12-year-old daughter at breakfast: "Do you think you're smarter than me?" — triggered by her wanting to brush teeth *after* eating instead of before.

**CS Lewis quote:** "Pride is spiritual cancer: it eats up the very possibility of love, or contentment, or common sense."

---

### Chapter 4 — Envy
**Core idea:** The $130 billion influencer marketing industry is built on one premise — make you want what someone else has. Envy is the alligator under the water. It rarely identifies itself. People admit to anger, lust, greed — but almost never envy. Yet envy secretly celebrates others' failures and resents others' blessings.

**Key verse:** Proverbs 14:30 — "A heart at peace gives life to the body, but envy rots the bones."

**Key illustration:** Social media as the "attention economy" — designed to trigger comparison.

**Historical illustration:** Hitler's exploitation of economic envy to turn neighbors against neighbors.

---

### Chapter 5 — Greed: The Fear of Never Enough
**Core idea:** $1.2 trillion in personal credit card debt. 23 million storage units in America (one for every 14 people). We live in a culture intoxicated by "more." Greed is almost impossible to see in yourself — you always think the "rich people" verses are for someone else. The deeper disease is a scarcity mentality: *there is never enough, and I must secure it myself.*

**Key verse:** Matthew 19:24 — "It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God." (Jesus says this because we can't see our own greed.)

---

### Chapter 6 — Lust
**Core idea:** Lust is far more than sexual desire — it is the desire to *consume*. Asherah poles (ancient symbols of sensual worship) are everywhere in modern culture. The digital age has weaponized lust. Matthew 5:28 — "Anyone who looks at a woman with lust has already committed adultery in his heart."

**Status:** Two AI-assisted outlines exist (ChatGPT and Gemini versions). No full manuscript chapter yet.

---

### Chapter 7 — Gluttony
**Core idea:** Defined as the consumption of *excess* of anything, not just food. Different from greed (which collects things) and lust (which desires things) — gluttony is about *more*. The author has personal connection through Hashimoto's disease (thyroid condition). The cultural rule says "food is comfort"; the new rule is "food is primarily fuel."

**Peter De Vries quote:** "Gluttony is an emotional escape, a sign that something is eating us."

**Status:** Thin draft/notes. Needs full chapter.

---

### Chapter 8 — Anger
**Core idea:** Anger reveals the heart. An explosion of anger isn't a slip — it's a revelation of what has been building. The author's blow-up at his oldest son: every word said had been thought for weeks. The filter just broke.

**Status:** Draft fragment. Needs development.

---

### Chapter 9 — Sloth (Acedia)
**Core idea:** Sloth isn't just laziness — it's *relational laziness*, spiritual laziness, and the refusal to do the hard inner work. Someone told Nic: "You're not lazy with your work — you're relationally lazy." Acedia is the ancient word: the noonday demon, the failure to care.

**Status:** Draft fragment. Needs development.

---

### Chapter 10 — Vainglory
**Core idea:** Vainglory is the desire for human applause — doing good things for the wrong audience. It's pride's close cousin. Matthew 6:1: "Be careful not to practice your righteousness in front of others to be seen by them." John 12:43: "They loved human praise more than praise from God."

**Status:** Outline and thin draft. May work as a fun, lighter chapter toward the end.

---

### Closing Chapter — Lake Placid Ends Where It Began
**Core idea:** The final chapter returns to the Lake Placid movie metaphor. The heroes kill the monster. But in the final shot, the sweet old lady is still feeding new gators at the water's edge. The victory was never over the alligator — it's over the *feeder*. Don't feed them. Whatever they're named — puppies, sugar, your little secret — we have become too comfortable with things that can kill us.

## The Chapter Template (Standard Structure)
Every chapter follows this pattern:

1. **ME** — A personal illustration from Nic's own life (vulnerability, not performance)
2. **WE** — Broadening to the universal human experience
3. **NAMING THE ALLIGATOR** — Defining this specific sin clearly
4. **THE EVER-GROWING GATOR** — How small it starts, how big it becomes
5. **GOD** — What Scripture says; theological grounding
6. **YOU** — 3 practical steps / reflection questions
7. **WE** (close) — Communal call to action; hope

## Key Thematic Threads (Running Throughout)

**The War Within** — The real battle is not political, relational, or circumstantial. It is internal. Paul in Romans 7 wrestles not with the world but with himself.

**Change the Rules, Change the Game** — Cultural rules keep us in bondage. God's rules (holiness, power, freedom) offer a different game. Dr. James Naismith invented basketball not to create a sport but to solve a problem. This is what the gospel does.

**Feed What You Want to Grow** — Whatever you feed grows; whatever you starve dies. We've spent years training our addictions and only trying to get better ourselves.

**Trying vs. Training** — There's a difference between *trying* to beat a sin and *training* to. Athletes don't try to win — they train for it. We've spent time training our issues and just trying to overcome them.

**Feelings Follow Faithfulness** — Your feelings will never lead you out. Only faithfulness does, and feelings eventually follow.

**We Are a Product of Our Patterns** — Change your patterns, change your future.

**Windows vs. Mirrors** — Most people look at the world through windows (seeing others' faults). The Bible is a mirror. Jordan Peterson: clean your own room before you tell others to clean theirs.
